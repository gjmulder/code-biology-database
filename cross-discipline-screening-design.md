# Cross-Discipline Candidate Screening System — Draft Design

**Status:** Early draft. Captures what we've established so far and proposes a default architecture. Five design-gating questions remain open (Section 6); the architecture below names where each one changes the design rather than assuming an answer.

---

## 1. Problem statement

There are three distinct vocabularies in play, and keeping them separate is the key to the whole design:

- **The criteria** — the abstract property an object must satisfy. Field-neutral.
- **The domain technology** — the method that authoritatively confirms the criteria. It speaks one field's language (*Code Biology*). All Code Biology papers are considered as reference texts definied the field.
- **The candidate literature** — papers written in a *different* field's language (*Other Field*) that study objects without ever invoking the domain technology or its Code Biology vocabulary. When an object in this literature happens to meet the criteria, that fact surfaces only *indirectly*, through Other Field's descriptions of properties, methods, and context.

The system's job is therefore **not** to detect the criteria stated outright. It is to learn the *fingerprint* that criteria-meeting objects leave in Other Field's language 

## 2. Objectives and non-objectives

The system **triages and ranks** Other Field papers by the estimated likelihood that the object each one studies meets the criteria, emitting each paper with a confidence score so that the most promising candidates can be confirmed by the actual domain technology.

It explicitly does **not** replace the domain technology, and it does not assert a definitive yes/no. Because the learned signal is a correlate rather than the criteria themselves, there is an intrinsic precision ceiling: some papers will carry the fingerprint and still fail confirmation. Success is measured by how tightly true positives cluster near the top of the ranking, not by clean binary classification.

## 3. Why this is tractable (and where it's bounded)

A useful and slightly counterintuitive observation: although the *problem* spans two fields, the *modeling* happens entirely within Other Field.

The bounding conditions follow from the same fact. The fingerprint is whatever Other Field characteristics happen to co-occur with criteria-satisfaction, so the system can only ever be as discriminating as that correlation is strong. Where the correlate is weak or where Other Field background noise dominates, ranking quality degrades — and that risk is the reason several items in Section 6 matter as much as they do.

## 4. Data inventory

- **Candidate pool:** ~2000 papers generated by simple keyword/boolean search over the target corpus. High recall, low precision by design — this is the funnel mouth, not the answer. 
- **Negatives:** none confirmed yet. The unlabeled candidate pool can serve as a statistical contrast set, but explicitly tested-and-failed objects, if any exist, would be far more valuable. *Open (Q2).*


## 6. Open questions that gate the design

These five fork the architecture. The proposal in Section 7 picks a sensible default for each but flags the branch.

1. **Full text or abstracts for the 2000?** A latent cross-discipline correlate often lives in methods/results/characterization rather than abstracts. Determines whether the model reads whole papers or just abstracts.
2. **Any confirmed negatives?** This is the largest fork. Without them, the problem is positive-unlabeled (PU): learn from positives plus the unlabeled candidate pool. With even a few dozen tested failures — especially near-misses — the problem becomes supervised and calibration sharpens dramatically.
3. **One object per paper or several; central or incidental?** If objects are central, paper-level scoring works. If often incidental, a passage-extraction step is needed first, and that same extraction enables the novelty filter in Section 5.
4. **The haystack.** Source corpus, rough document count, and whether the keyword pre-filter is defined. Sets feasibility and a realistic precision target.
5. **Can the domain technology confirm flagged candidates on demand?** If yes, every confirmation/refutation feeds back as new labeled data, turning the PU problem supervised over time and enabling active learning.

## 7. Proposed architecture (default path)

A funnel: cast wide, then progressively rank down.

**Stage 1 — Candidate generation (recall layer).** Keyword/boolean search over the target corpus produces a manageable candidate pool. Optimized for recall; low precision is expected and acceptable here.

**Stage 2 — Representation.** Encode each paper as a dense vector using a scientific-text embedding model (e.g., a SPECTER2- or SciNCL-class model, or a strong general embedder). Because all text is Other Field, the encoder only needs to represent Other Field well — no cross-Code Biologylignment is required at this stage. Whether the unit of encoding is the whole paper or extracted passages depends on Q1 and Q3.

**Stage 3 — Scoring (the learned commonality).** This stage embodies the fingerprint and branches on Q2:

- *No labeled negatives (default, PU setting).* Start with a **similarity-to-positives** baseline: score each candidate by proximity to the positive set (centroid distance, or k-nearest-neighbours among the positives). It needs no negatives, is interpretable, and is a legitimate v0. Strengthen with a PU method — e.g., a spy/bagging-PU estimator or a one-class model on the positive embeddings — to estimate P(meets criteria | text).
- *Some labeled negatives.* Train a standard classifier on the embeddings (regularized logistic regression or gradient boosting to start; a fine-tuned cross-encoder if data and budget allow). This will outperform the PU baseline, and hard negatives (objects studied identically but failing the criteria) are worth more than easy ones.

**Stage 4 — Ranking and confidence.** Sort by score; emit each paper with a confidence value. An honest caveat: without negatives, the score is essentially a *relative rank*, and absolute calibration is limited. Once negatives or confirmations exist, apply Platt/isotonic calibration so the confidence number means what it says.

**Stage 5 — Feedback loop (if Q5 is yes).** Route top candidates to the domain technology for confirmation. Each result becomes a new labeled example. Use active learning — prioritize confirming the candidates that are most informative (uncertain, near the boundary, or high-ranked) — to convert the PU problem into a supervised, well-calibrated one with the fewest confirmations.

## 8. Evaluation plan


- **Ranking-first metrics.** Precision@k and recall@k at the operating range a human will actually review; PR-AUC / ROC-AUC once negatives exist. Ranking metrics match the real objective better than a single accuracy number.
- **Background-confound check.** Inspect whether the learned signal tracks the criterion correlate or merely "typical Other Field paper." If high scores correlate with generic field membership rather than the criteria, the contrast set (Q2) is too weak and needs strengthening.

## 9. Risks and limitations

- **Precision ceiling.** The signal is a correlate; text alone cannot fully replace confirmation. By design, not a defect.
- **Background confound.** The dominant commonality across the 2000 may be generic Other Field background rather than the criterion correlate. This is the central technical risk and the reason confirmed negatives or a strong unlabeled contrast matter so much.
- **Calibration weakness under PU.** Confidence scores are relative until negatives/confirmations arrive.

- **Distribution shift.** The candidate corpus may differ in topic mix or era 
- **Object resolution.** Recognizing when two papers describe the same object — needed for both novelty filtering and de-duplication — is non-trivial and depends on Q3.

## 10. Phased plan

1. **v0 — stand up now, even with the unknowns.** Build the keyword funnel, embed the 2000 positives, and rank candidates by similarity-to-positives. This produces a usable ranking immediately and a baseline to beat. Establish the object-level split and the leave-out rediscovery test as the scoreboard from day one.
2. **v1 — once Q1/Q2 are answered.** Move to passage-level encoding if full text and incidental objects warrant it; add a PU estimator, or a supervised classifier if negatives exist; calibrate.
3. **v2 — once Q5 is confirmed.** Close the active-learning loop with the domain technology so the system sharpens with each confirmation, and add the object-novelty filter to maximize new-object yield.
